Nanoscience and Materials

Chemistry is a study of matter, and how it interacts with surrounding or another matters. As a chemist we learned about the properties of different molecules and interaction with the surroundings. Some chemist are specialized in chemical-chemical interactions to produce a new desired chemical, but I was more focused on the fundamental properties of the chemicals and the matter constructed from the chemicals. For my graduate degree, I studied fundamental properties of metal-containing nanomaterials orginating from subtle differences in their material structures. Now, My current work involves on producing better performance and cost effective materials containing carbon electrodes and polymer membranes for their practical applications.


Theories

To design and control the properties of new materials, it is extremely impotant to understand the chemical/physical theory behind the materials. Using the previously existing theories can be used to create a model material, then the model is compared to what is observed in the laboratory. By comparison and testing, we can make understand the origin of the observed phenomena, or to fill the gap in the theory. While my current work does not involve heavy theoratical side of the materials we produce, I'm always interested in the fundamental theories that governs the properties of the materials.

Masters Research

My masters studies was under Dr. Millstone in University of Pittsburgh. Millstone Group focuses on the properties of nanomaterials. Among numerous interesting nanomaterials, my masters work was focused on small gold nanoparticles and gold nanocrystals. Studying their syntheses, properties, and the theories behind them.

Summer Research

Summer after my sophomore year, I had a chance to work under Dr. Yaron in Carnegie Mellon University. Yaron Group focuses on the theoretical description of the photophysics of conjugated polymers and other organic semiconductors While I was lacking in my understanding of modern computational theories in chemistry, Dr. Yaron helped me to work on understanding and describing electronic models in rather comlicated systems using different calculation methods including density functional tight binding (DFTB) methods.
