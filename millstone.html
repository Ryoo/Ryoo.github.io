Very Small Nanoparticles

The word 'nano' originates from the ancient greek word 'nanos,' meaning 'dwarf.' It is a prefix to describe something extremely small, and the unit nanometer is a billion times smaller than the meter stick. Therefore, nanoparticles are particles that have at least one dimension smaller than 100 nm. My work in the Millstone group focused on much smaller nanoparticles, i.e., pseudospherical nanoparticles that are smaller than 20nm, especially those less than 5 nm in all dimensions. At these small dimensions, materials start to behave different than they would behave in bulk systems. One of the most famous example of the many unique nanomaterial properties is localized surface plasmon resonance (LSPR). This is when the nanoparticle is smaller than the wavelength of the light, thus electron clouds in the nanoparticle oscillate with the incoming lights, producing specific colors back. The oscillation of the electron clouds can be controlled by using different wavelengths of light, but is also due to the size and shape of the nanoparticles. In my work, we focused on the surface structures of the nanoparticles and how modification to the surface translated to overall nanomaterial properties.

Nanocrystals with various surface properties

My research was mainly focused on gold nanocrystals. The nanocrystals are even smaller versions of the nanoparticles, but they have defined crystal structures. Their crystal structures can be collected via X-ray diffractometer after multiple purifications and crystalizations. Having a defined structure enables theoratical modelings to study more definitive connections between observation and theory. My work focused on synthesizing nanocrystals with 11 gold core (undecanoic gold clusters) and 13 gold core (icosahedral gold clusters) with varying ligands. These gold cores were stabilized with L-type ligands (pnictides) and X-type ligands (halogens). From elucidating their crystal structures to measuring their absorption and emission properties, the similarities and differences were studied by making subtle modifications to their surface.

Modeling of the Nanocrystals

With a known crystal structure, the coordinates of each atom can be used to build a computational model. Computational models are used to calculate electronic structures from existing theories. By comparing the electronic structures of similar nanocrystals with different surface ligands, we can observe how surface ligands can perturb the super atomic orbitals of the gold core. Additionally, the experimental observations can be compared to the electronic structures of the same nanocrystal models to inform the origin of the phenomena that is observed. Mild influences of the surface ligands on the properties of the nanocrystals, such as absorptions and reactivity, are described by similarities of the electronic structures. On the other hand, certain new properties heavily influenced by the surface ligands, like emission strength and stabilities, are suggested by the differences of the electronic structures. This work aims to understand ligand-nanomaterial interactions, and aid in design of new generation nanomaterials.
